Does Sydney have cleaner air than Melbourne?

Author

Varun Sridhar, Suppawish Siriamnat

Published

October 13, 2024

Air quality is a critical environmental with numerous contaminants, each of which has the potential to directly impact both the environment and human health, have an impact on air quality. The following contaminants raise different health concerns and are important markers of air quality:

•⁠ ⁠PM2.5: Particulate particles less than 2.5 microns in diameter are referred to as PM2.5. These tiny particles can enter the lungs deeply, leading to respiratory and cardiovascular issues.

•⁠ ⁠PM10: Particulate matter smaller than ten microns in diameter. These particles can still be harmful to health because they irritate the respiratory system, even if they are larger than PM2.5.

•⁠ ⁠Sulfur Dioxide (SO2): This gas is mostly created during industrial processes when fossil fuels are burned. It can irritate the respiratory system and aid in the development of acid rain.

•⁠ ⁠Ozone (O3): An atmospheric gas that is hazardous below ground level but helpful above. One of the main causes of smog is ground-level ozone, which aggravates respiratory conditions.

•⁠ ⁠⁠Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2): A contaminant emitted mostly by car emissions and industrial activity. Long-term exposure can cause lung function to decline and cause respiratory issues.

•⁠ ⁠⁠Carbon Monoxide (CO): An odorless, colorless gas that is often released from cars when fossil fuels are not completely burned. Serious health hazards can arise from high CO levels because they can hinder the body’s capacity to supply oxygen to organs.

Understanding air quality and evaluating these pollutants helps evaluate whether Sydney has cleaner air than Melbourne during July and August 2024. By comparing the parameters’ measurements between the two cities, we can determine air quality differences and the quality of each city on its own. However, there is a limitation of different amount of locations in each city and different pollutant parameter that could impact this analysis.

Aggregating data

For these data, the measurements are like time stamps, and pollutants are not recorded at the same instant. It is necessary to aggregate the data to the hourly level for each parameter and location. To aggregate the data at the hourly level, we need to group the pollutant measurements by both time and location, then we can calculate the average value of each pollutant for that hour and location.

Code 
melbourne_aggregate <- melbourne_data |> 
  mutate(time = hms::as_hms(date_utc)) |> 
  group_by(location, parameter, time, unit, lat, long) |> 
  summarize(value = mean(value, na.rm = TRUE), .groups = "drop")

sydney_aggregate <- sydney_data |> 
  mutate(time = hms::as_hms(date_utc)) |> 
  group_by(location, parameter, time, unit, lat, long) |> 
  summarize(value = mean(value, na.rm = TRUE), .groups = "drop")

Imputing missing values for the missing time gap

Imputing the data for the missing time gap of each parameter that have data recorded for each location in the data set. Imputation is done by using the moving average method with the width window of 4 observations (2 observations before and after).

Code 
mel_wide <- melbourne |> 
  select(location, time, parameter, value) |> 
  pivot_wider(id_cols = c(location, time),
              names_from = parameter,
              values_from = value) 

syd_wide <- sydney_tsibble |> 
  select(location, time, parameter, value) |> 
  pivot_wider(id_cols = c(location, time),
              names_from = parameter,
              values_from = value) |> 
  mutate(co = NA,
         no2 = NA,
         o3 = NA,
         so2 = NA) |> 
  select(location, time, co, no2, o3, pm10, pm25, so2)
Code 
alphington <- mel_wide |>

  mutate(co = na_ma(co),

Temporal Patterns

From Figure 6, the temporal patterns can be observed for each of the locations in different pollutant parameters.

  • PM2.5 sharp spikes: Sudden increases in PM2.5 levels are observed during mid-day, especially in Sydney and Melbourne CBD.

  • SO2 spike in Alphington: A noticeable rise in SO2 levels occurs in the morning, followed by a fall in the evening in Alphington.

  • O3 (Ozone) anomaly: Ozone levels in Melbourne CBD are consistently higher compared to other locations during early morning.

  • NO2 and CO divergence: The trends for NO2 and CO differ across various locations, with some areas showing larger increases in NO2.

  • Time-based fluctuations in PM10: PM10 levels fluctuate more during certain times of the day, particularly in Dandenong in the late day.

Comparing air quality of Melbourne and Sydney

Considering the pollutant parameters to analyze to compare the air quality of Melbourne and Sydney, Sydney only contains two parameters which are PM10 and PM2.5 which are the same as parameters in Melbourne. Therefore, we will use these two parameters to compare the air quality of the two cities.

PM10 Levels in Sydney vs Melbourne:

From Figure 9:

  • Sydney:

    • Consistently lower 10 µg/m³ PM10 levels, peaking around 15 µg/m³ at midday.

    • PM10 levels decline steadily after the peak, indicating relatively cleaner air.

  • Melbourne (Alphington, Brooklyn, Dandenong, Footscray, Mooroolbark):

    • Brooklyn has the highest PM10 levels exceeding 30 µg/m³ in the morning, then decreasing during the day and spiking again at night.

    • Alphington shows a morning peak of almost 20 µg/m³ followed by a decline, but with a smaller late-day recovery.

    • Dandenong and Footscray exhibit moderate PM10 levels, fluctuating between 12 and 16 µg/m³, peaking at midday.

    • Mooroolbark shows lower PM10 levels, with a peak around midday close to 15 µg/m³.

Sydney generally experiences lower PM10 levels compared to most Melbourne locations, suggesting cleaner air. In contrast, Melbourne areas, particularly Brooklyn and Alphington, experience higher and more volatile PM10 levels, indicating more significant air quality issues. Moreover, when looking at the summary statistic in Table 6, all of the statistics of PM10 level in Sydney are lower compared to all locations in Melbourne.

Final conclusion

When comparing the air quality of Sydney and Melbourne during July and Ausgust 2024 using PM10 and PM2.5 levels, Sydney generally has cleaner air, especially in terms of PM10. The PM10 levels in Sydney are consistently lower and more stable throughout the day, peaking at lower values compared to Melbourne. In Melbourne, areas like Brooklyn and Alphington have much higher and more unpredictable PM10 levels, often exceeding double the levels seen in Sydney. This indicates that Sydney experiences less air pollution from PM10 compared to many parts of Melbourne.

For PM2.5, Sydney experiences spikes in PM2.5 levels during the day similar to high-pollution areas in Melbourne, such as Alphington and Brighton, where levels reach around 12 µg/m³. However, other locations in Melbourne, such as Brooklyn, Macleod, and Footscray, show more stable and lower PM2.5 levels, between 5-8 µg/m³. Moreover, during the time period when Sydney has a low value, the lowest value of PM2.5 that Sydney has is lower than most of the locations in Melbourne except Footscray and Macleod. This indicates that while Sydney’s PM2.5 levels are higher at certain times of the day, some areas in Melbourne maintain better air quality for PM2.5 but Melbourne still can’t compare during the lower value at certain times of the day.

In conclusion, during July and Ausgust 2024, Sydney tends to have cleaner air than Melbourne especially when comparing the PM10 level. The PM2.5 level in Sydney is still comparable to the PM2.5 level in Melbourne locations, as the minimum PM2.5 level in Sydney is lower than the minimum level in multiple locations of Melbourne but the maximum is also higher than all Melbourne’s locations.
